Design Problem Statement

For the Design Competition, the assigned task of designing and creating an autonomous “vacuum cleaner” was given, with the goal of picking up and storing as much debris as possible by weight. The competition fixture was an arena made of conjoined 2x4 lumber with walls approximately 3.5 inches high. The arena area was 64 square feet. Inside the arena was debris of various sizes, shapes, and materials such as sand, marbles, metal screws, and styrofoam balls. Each team competed in head-to-head competitions with the winner being the team that collected the most debris by weight. The overall competition was double elimination.

Figure 1: Competition arena with debris and two devices competing

In order to be eligible to compete, each team had to satisfy a series of requirements as outlined by the Design Competition Description. These included the following:

  • The device’s initial size must not exceed 30 cm (~12 inches) in length nor in width. There is no restriction on height.
  • The device must take one action (press button, flip switch, etc.) to initialize the device. The action may be manual or remote, but there cannot be any further contact with the device after initialization.
  • Each team is allowed a maximum total budget of $100 for this project.
  • The entire device must remain within the arena boundaries throughout the race.

Target Customers

The Target Customers are people who want a more hands free option to cleaning messes that have larger debris such as sand and marbles. If it’s a workspace, home or office, SARAI is able to provide automated cleaning. Beneficial for those lacking the time to clean these sorts of messes or those who might no longer be able to do so. Easy to empty and operate, this device is appealing to customers who don’t need overly complicated systems in their lives.

This auto backup after 5 loops also helps ensure that large piles of debris do not cause a current surge and activate built-in safeguards that shut down the device. Research Description

The research involved looking into products that fulfilled the primary requirement of being a vacuum through conventional suction or alternative methods of picking up debris. This base research gave way to specific products, which inspired more lines of research. From here, dissection guides such as Consumer Reports provided even more insight into features and requirements that would be important to consider and prioritize in the development of the robots. Some of these additional requirements to consider were durability, environment as well as object pickup versatility [1].

On the note of specific requirements, once those were identified, additional research could be done by looking into devices that were the best in these specific categories. For example, looking at environment versatility the Shark Upright was one of the devices that excelled in this category [5]. Looking at outside reviews ensured that at least some bias was removed surrounding the product. From there, looking at device specifications to get a gauge of the capability of the vacuum without purchasing or performing any in person tests.

Something more familiar was the dustpan and broom, and was less research based and an understanding of the workings relied more on experience with a dustpan and broom. Because iRobot’s Roomba was one of the only identified competitors that was automated it was of utmost importance to diligently research and identify components of importance to both the manufacturers of Roomba’s as well as the customers. Looking into all the components of the automated robot was vital in understanding what mechanics and interfaces were critical [6].

This research was also of utmost importance to making the decision to focus on an automated rolling dustpan design rather than an automated convention suction based vacuum design. Making for inquisitive questions such as what makes a vacuum have suction, what roller designs are best in vacuums and power consumption of average devices in order to build off of [7]. 

Specification Development

In determining the specifications it was important to take a step back and analyze all the research that was collected and look for common threads. These were indicators of what was important across the board to consumers. The specifications developed here and shown on both the QFD and Customer Requirements also incorporated competition constraints.

First, there were physical specifications which were naturally first in the development as it was easiest to visualize based off seeing other vacuums, envision SARAI’s concept and the easiest to plan around. These physical specifications included the 1ft x 1ft constraint as well as a light product, weighing under 10 lbs. One of the stand out features of the device that needed to be created was that it was autonomous. Looking at what popular autonomous robots used to base specifications around the possible ideas for making the robot autonomous was an important first step [8].

From there the natural progression was to the electrical components that naturally segwayed into safety for the device. The specifications surrounding safety included, wiring competency, obstacle detection and a safe and reliable power supply. Some of the specifics here are highlighted in the following figure.

Pivoting now toward the customer and how the device could be best made in their interest, more specifications were developed. Here it was important to consider the ease of cleaning, the minimality of upkeep, a full circle back to ease of transport and lastly an interface that started from only one switch.

Specification Priority

There were constraints that had automatic priority, because if these were not met then the robot would not be able to compete. However after that, the top priority was given to only one specification grouping: Variety of Environment and Debris. This category held the two key features that would allow the robot to have a chance at winning; its ability to move and the brush rotation. Without both of these components there was no way anything would be collected.

The second layer to these was that if the robot could move but not in competition that constraint was null and void- same applied to the brush spinning but not being able to move any debris to the collection bin. These priority specifications are outlined on the following figure.

Specification Tradeoffs

What becomes difficult with the specifications is focusing and improving one aspect can have negative effects on others. The QFD analysis helped to determine some areas of conflict. By then prioritizing these specs, it can be determined which tradeoffs are worth making while designing the device. Initially when the first concepts included a fan/suction based vacuum design these compromised the ease of maintenance as well as safety.

However, with the improved design having more torque in the wheels to increase the versatility of movement- meant the batteries would not last as long. Having the brush rotate to collect material impacted the speed of the vacuum as it had to move slow enough to allow collection into the bin and not create a pile of sand, thus causing current to trip the motors.

Specification Targets and Thresholds

The limits and thresholds set were based on the initial research into industry standards. Based on this tried and true method, having thresholds around these values allowed the device to not have to reinvent the wheel. Examples of these were the power consumption which was based on vacuum averages and the noise output based on human ear health.

Other targets were based on the general guidelines provided for the competition to make ideal device specifications such as the speed needed to clean the entire arena in the allotted time. Lastly, these competition requirements inspired many of the thresholds in terms of durability. Imagining best case scenarios for the competition was what determined power needed, batteries and device longevity.

Concept Selection

The process that was used in order to design the device was to first start with the functionality of the device. Three main features were identified: that it could move, that it could move in a ‘smart’ way, and that it could collect different types of debris. By starting here, the team was able to identify what was needed from the device.

After spending time coming up with ideas individually, the team met and put together the Morphology template. The three main specifications relating to the competition and how the device could perform well were: collecting different types of debris (marbles vs sand) and how to move/turn efficiently. The top three designs for each of these specifications was chosen and then evaluated against competitors and in general. From this and research done on vacuums, it was decided to go with a broom and dustpan design instead of a traditional vacuum. The morphology template was created for vacuums, but the best features were still incorporated into the prototype and final design. Such as having a rotating brush at the front to pick up marbles and sand, as well as having two wheels to make turning more efficient. From there, each component was perfected to make it as efficient as possible. The process for making the components efficient is written below.

An important component for the device is having powerful and effective wheels. Since the front end of the vacuum will be sliding against the surface, there was a clear need to have wheels that can provide enough turning force to get the vacuum to continue moving against resistance. Additionally, it was important to consider how to make the vacuum pivot when collision is imminent. With the two wheels, it would require the power to each wheel be controlled with separate motors to allow for one wheel to stop quickly and pivot away from obstacles. To detect an imminent collision, the team decided that having ultrasonic sensors would be the appropriate choice for this issue. When the ultrasonic sensors detect an obstacle a certain distance away, the program will allow the device to pivot quickly.

The front brush is also powered by a motor to allow it to be powerful enough to collect sand and the larger debris. The device’s brush pushes the sand and other debris over a small lip inside of the vacuum. After overcoming this small lip, the larger debris can roll down a slope with gravity into the collection bin. The brush motor also needed to be powerful enough to overcome the friction resistance from the bristles brushing against the ground and ramp. To collect sand the bristles needed to reach out past the edge of the device while still dragging against the ground to be able to collect sand. If the bristles overlapped with the ground too much it would become stuck and not be able to overcome the resistance. The height of the brush was a balance that was found mostly through trial and error of running the device and adjusting the height accordingly.

Design Organization: Group 5

Product Decomposed: S.A.R.A.I. AUTOMATED VACUUM

Description: This is an automated vacuum designed with the intention of cleaning a predefined area containing various objects including sand, screws and marbles. With one button push, it should be able to function without any other user input until the cleaning job is completed.

How it Works: The SARAI automated vacuum utilizes ultrasonic sensors to navigate its surroundings and features a rotating brush at the front to capture debris. Specifically, this brush is powered by a geared motor on either side of the 3D printed cylinder and these motors rotate from a battery power supply. The cylinder is 3D printed and has bristles glued along the body in order to capture debris and move it to the collection bin.  There are two wheels on the side of the body of the device and these are powered by 6V motors. These wheels are programmed to move based on surroundings with alternating power supply in order to turn and navigate their enclosure.

Movement Sequence and Steps

The SARAI device implements a simple yet effective design that pays homage to the dust and broom pan cleaning method. The device is designed to drive in a straight line, with the front wheel spinning to intake debris, until the ultrasonic sensors detect some object is in its path (other vacuum or arena barrier). When these sensors detect some object in its path, it is programmed to reverse at half speed for 5 seconds before turning 120 degrees either left or right (depending on which sensor was triggered first). Then it continues driving forward until the sensors are triggered again.

Another thing that the device implements is that after 5 iterations of the loop, the device will automatically back up and pivot. This was added as a second factor of safety in the event that one of the sensors breaks or in the event that too much debris gets collected on the edge of the collection bin. This gives the device a way to get out of a potentially round ending situation.

Early in development it was found that the device auto shuts off if too much resistance is detected, the previously mentioned factor of safety feature keeps the device out of precarious situations such as this.

A step-by-step sequence of movement steps is outlined below:

  1. Device is manually switched on
  2. Current flows to the arduino which in turn activates the setup and loop code
  3. The arduino tells device to move forward at predetermined speed and turns on the brushes motor on
  4. Device detects object in its path
  5. Device backs up for 5 seconds at half speed
  6. Device pivots 120 degrees either left or right
  7. Device checks for if the iterations are divisible by 5, if so it repeats steps 5 and 6 before continuing.
  8. Process is repeated until switch is turned off

        The SARAI device has been designed to consider the quantity of sand in the arena and is built to quickly intake sand. When too much sand is in the way, it’s capable of adjusting angle and attack path to attempt a different approach. The device is able to function effectively for a short period of time, so the failsafe was implemented to increase the duration the device is capable of operating for. More complex designs exist, but simplicity isn’t something to be overlooked. If it functions and works effectively, that is what matters more.

Engineering Analysis

One of the ways the team’s design incorporates engineering knowledge is in the use of statics/dynamics to understand how S.A.R.A.I. will move. While statics deals with objects and systems in equilibrium, this design must focus on making sure a changing weight load on the device won’t cause it to topple or to not be able to move.

When dealing with the motors that are used to power both the wheels and the rotating brush, some engineering knowledge that was leaned on was how to determine correct power supplied to the motors and what sort of resistor would be needed if there was a specific battery power supply. In addition, the team assessed what sort of circuit to build (whether the motors should be run in parallel or in series) and how to connect all the components to ensure the internal wiring would be proficient and assure longevity out of the components.

Knowing how to read the specifications of motors was also important in ensuring that they were used properly and not being incorrectly wired or pushed beyond their capacity leading to avoidable damage.

Figure 10: Mock circuits including all the motor and sensor components

 Knowledge of SolidWorks was helpful to create a model of the device and to see how the parts fit together as an assembly and also came in use when preparing the device files to be 3D printed. By designing models with the intent to 3D print them, it became easier in the construction process to build and create the product.

During the research process, several types of products were found that could complete the requirements of the product. The pros and cons of each device were analyzed, as well as comparing the process through which each completes its required tasks. Ultimately, the executive decision was made to mimic the actions of a broom and dustpan with the structure of an automated vacuum. Additionally, one important consideration the group wished to attend to was the implementation of effective cost analysis strategies. Before purchasing each part, we ran it by other online suppliers to see if we were getting the “best bang for our buck” so as to not break the bank with our design. By price checking, more money was left over in the event that something unexpected came up or something was required for design that wasn’t originally considered during the early design phase. This ability to have room to pivot and adjust is important but hard to get right during the design process.

Very early on in the design process, knowledge of Fluids Dynamics came into play when assessing the effort and technique needed to make a conventional sucking vacuum.

Figure 11: Vacuum showcasing the Fluids Dynamics theory that makes suction

Here an understanding of pressure gradients and motion of a fluid in pressure gradients was key to making an educated decision on if the group would be pursuing a traditional vacuum design or sticking with the more atypical moving dust pan.

A major difficulty faced with the motors was ensuring the motors produced enough torque to output usable work. Originally, the motors were outputting high revolutions per minute (rpm), but these motors were not able to turn the wheels or brush. This is because the torque produced by these motors was too low to allow movement. To fix this issue, motors with gearboxes were used to power both wheels and the front brush. Gearboxes use gear ratios to create useful torque from revolutions per minute. A gearbox is made up of a smaller gear connected to the shaft of the motor. The smaller gear is interlocked with a larger gear that turns at half the speed of the smaller gear. The larger gear produces more torque as it has the same force applied over a greater radius (distance). The large gear is then connected to the output shaft that turns the wheels or brush [9].

Hazards and High Levels of Risk

From the FTA- areas of risk that need to be focused on are one of the critical elements not working. If the device neither moves or has a rotating brush then there isn’t any chance to collect debris, thus knocking the ability to compete. Due to the critical nature of these elements it’s important to analyze all possible scenarios that lead to these failures in order to best prepare.

Improvements

        One issue that was discovered during the testing of the device was that the device would run into large piles of sand and not be able to continue moving forward. This would cause a current surge in the device and due to safety features of the motors, the device would shut down. It was decided to change the coding so that the device would move forward for one second and then backwards for one second before moving forward again to prevent large piles accumulating in front of the device. On competition day, this code was removed since the competition arena debris amount was not as dense as the testing arena debris amount. After discussion with Nelson in the Mechatronics lab, it was also determined that buck converters would assist with this issue. These converters step down the voltage from the input power source (while increasing current), and the amount of voltage going to the component is adjusted [10]. Therefore, if a 9 V power supply is connected to one of the motors, the buck converter allows this voltage to be adjusted to 6 V and that is what the component will receive. This allows the components to run at a voltage they are rated for and therefore increase the lifespan and safety of the component.


Figure 19: Buck converters on the device

The change in coding to back the device up and move forward to avoid debris accumulation brought another problem. When moving backward, the device would rock backward on its rear wheels causing the front to raise up in the air. The forward motion brought the front back down again, however this issue was aggravated when there was debris in the collection bin which caused the weight of the device to stay in the back and not bring the front back down. The improvement for this was to change the speed of the reverse motion and to add some weight to the front of the device. We were given a piece of steel stock by the CSU EMEC which we placed right on top of the device near the front.


Figure 20: The steel stock added to aid with balance issues

The team also had concerns with the amount of wires that were initially used on the device. At one point, four Arduino RedBoards were used for the device which resulted in a tangle of wiring. The buck converters also helped with this issue as the device now only required one RedBoard so the amount of wiring was able to be drastically reduced.

Failure Analysis

        Failing is never a good feeling and while the vacuum created did perform well (4th place), it wasn’t top 3. Due to this, it’s important to take a retrospective look at what could’ve been changed in this process. Anything from day one ideas to flicking the on switch in the final match is fair game. This was an opportunity to consider why things transpired in the fashion which they did.

        First and foremost, lots of time was spent in the ideation phase. While this was important to make sure a starting point was formed, the actual creation of our device was delayed. If the construction on the device had begun sooner, some of the smaller issues that eventually arose would’ve been resolved quicker. For example, several days were spent designing the device around small motors that were ultimately scrapped. Had construction of this device started sooner, this would’ve been noticed faster allowing more time to test. This extra time could’ve also allowed for more testing on the code which still wasn’t operating at what would be considered the most optimal speed.

        Second, batteries were a huge issue during the design process. Not only were several days spent getting the batteries to a functioning voltage, but quick drops in the power supplied to the device after use was incredibly troublesome. More energy should’ve been devoted to researching power, for example a 12V battery was found a few days prior to the competition. After the event, some time was spent messing with a larger battery and it was found that it fixed some of the issues that arose from using 9V batteries. The battery upgrade would’ve helped the brush rotate more, which could’ve given the device more of an edge in the competition. It wasn’t included in the competition because it would’ve been too large of a change at the last minute, but had more research and testing been put into the batteries earlier on, it would’ve been able to be implemented.

        Finally, issues cropped up with the ultrasonic sensors. While it was unclear during the competition, testing afterwards suggested that the sensors both got overloaded with current due to swapping the ends of the positive and negative terminals right before competition. While a second safety mechanism was implemented in the event like this would occur, the time lost while competing waiting for this failsafe to kick in could’ve been the difference between a loss and a win. Two things could’ve been implemented to resolve this issue. Either improve the housing of the device so that if a wire gets displaced, it’s clear where it belongs to put it back in while in a hurry. Or alternatively, the failsafe could’ve been programmed to activate sooner, so less time would be spent in a corner waiting for the random pivot to activate.

        While other slight issues might’ve existed in the device, these were the main issues that had they been resolved or looked at sooner, could’ve drastically changed the course of the design process or competition. Some other small changes to the device and the design process that could have pushed the device closer to the podium are listed below.

  1. Add more sensors to increase the device's object permanence.
  2. Spend more time researching motors ahead of time to settle on a better one sooner.
  3. Rely less on custom 3D printed parts and more on components that could be easily acquired and swapped.

        Despite not placing in the top three, fourth place is nothing to scoff at. The device originally envisioned during the design process ultimately came to fruition and despite some minor flaws, the product was ultimately competition ready and was able to go several rounds before finally being outperformed. Lots of hard work went into this device and it shows in the final design and its capabilities.
